# Three-minute pitch — Theseus

> A practice script for the founder. Five beats, three minutes total.
> Restrained, confident, not promotional. No "AI-powered", no
> "revolutionary", no emoji. Speak in plain sentences.

---

## 1. The recognition that built the last great firm (30s)

Renaissance Technologies started from a single recognition: that
**price data has patterns worth arbitraging** — not the patterns
fundamental analysts were looking at, but the statistical patterns
underneath them, the regularities you only see if you have the
discipline to abstract numerical structure out of noise.

They built a machine for that recognition: data archive, model
trainer, fitted parameters, live signal, position. The machine ran
the same shape every day, on every market, for forty years.

## 2. The recognition that builds the next one (45s)

We started from the next recognition up the stack: that **logic has
patterns worth arbitraging.** Not the price layer — the layer above
it. Inside a good text, there are stable shapes: principles,
expressible in a sentence or two, that organise how an attentive
reader maps the situation to a position. Those principles are
recurring; they apply to situations the original author never saw;
they predict things the chart only describes after the fact.

We are the **Renaissance Technologies of formal logic**. The
machine has the same shape — corpus instead of tick data,
synthesizer instead of model trainer, principle instead of fitted
parameter, algorithm instead of strategy code, conclusion instead of
position. One layer up.

## 3. A concrete example: the arms-race algorithm (45s)

Here is one of our algorithms in plain language.

The **principle**, extracted from the corpus: when two parties enter
an arms race and one signals a credible escalation while the other
is institutionally constrained from matching it, the conflict
converges on a settlement on terms favourable to the unconstrained
party — not on the open conflict the news coverage is pricing.

The **observation**, last quarter: party A signalled a credible
escalation; party B's institutional constraints were already on the
record; the prediction markets and the headlines were pricing open
conflict.

The **algorithm fires.** It writes the conclusion: settlement on
A-favourable terms inside the horizon. It implies the bet: long the
settlement contract, short the conflict contract, in the same
window. The portfolio agent sizes it. The bet is recorded. When the
horizon closes, we grade ourselves against what actually happened —
and the principle's weighting goes up or down accordingly.

Apply the principle. Predict the bet. That is the whole shape.

## 4. The bet is polymorphic (30s)

The machine is **bet-domain-polymorphic**. Same corpus, same
principle library, same algorithm registry — different venues:

- **Equities** — for theses with multi-quarter horizons.
- **Prediction markets** — discrete questions, settlement dates,
  the calibration backbone.
- **Advisory** — the same conclusions, sold as judgement.
- **Scientific** — hypotheses the principle library predicts.
- **Private markets**, eventually, once the calibration record on
  the public venues is long enough to justify the cycle time.

The principle does not care which venue prices it. The machine just
ports.

## 5. The machine is our edge (30s)

We are not selling the machine. There is no SaaS plan. A
distribution would force one workflow on every buyer and expose the
corpus, the principle library, and the algorithm internals to anyone
with a credit card. The machine is valuable in proportion to how
much of our specific reading is encoded into it — and that value
collapses the moment it ships outside the firm.

We **invest with the machine.** We do not sell access to it. The
public surfaces — Currents, Forecasts, Memos, Algorithms,
Principles, the knowledge graph — are the firm thinking in public.
They are evidence. They are not the product.

The machine is our edge. We are raising capital to run it.
