\section{Why a separate operator role?}

The firm runs on \emph{separation of duties}. Any founder can upload
material, browse the corpus, review conclusions, ask the Oracle, and
read everything the firm publishes. A small number of actions ---
publishing to the public site, signing the quarterly methodology
review, flipping kill switches, authorizing real-money trades,
removing material the firm has cited publicly, managing roles and
credentials --- are concentrated in the operator role
(``founder-alpha'').

This is a deliberate constraint, for three reasons.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{Real-world consequences.} Publishing and trading touch
        the world outside the firm. Each of those gates wants a single
        accountable actor.
  \item \textbf{Cryptographic signing.} The firm signs its public
        publications and its quarterly review with private keys. Those
        keys live with the operator role, not in the web app, so
        signing is an explicit human action.
  \item \textbf{Concentration of risk.} Kill switches and live-bet
        authorization can lose money. They live on the surface that
        gets reviewed most carefully.
\end{itemize}

Throughout the rest of this guide, ``founder-alpha'' is the operator
role. If you are a general founder, treat this guide as a map of what
happens to your work after you hand it off.

\section{The operations dashboards}

The operations card on the dashboard is the operator's first stop when
something seems off. It shows:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item Each scheduler loop and its last successful cycle time.
  \item Recent error counts per loop.
  \item The state of each ingestion path (Currents X ingestion, the
        articles dispatcher, the forecasts pipeline).
  \item Auto-processing health (whether uploads sitting in
        \texttt{queued\_offline} are being picked up).
  \item Embedding-backfill state.
\end{itemize}

The operator drills in through dedicated sub-pages for CI status,
load metrics (request volume, latency percentiles, error rate per
method), and retention policies in effect.

\section{The publication review queue}

\page{Publication} is the gate before any conclusion or article goes
public. The queue advances through five states: \texttt{queued $\to$
in\_review $\to$ needs\_revision $\to$ declined $\to$ published}.

Each queue item presents a \textbf{checklist} founder-alpha works
through before approving. The checklist captures:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{Meta-analysis OK} --- the conclusion's methodology
        profile has been read and is acceptable.
  \item \textbf{Adversarial engaged OK} --- the adversarial cycle has
        run and the strongest objection has been reviewed (not just
        produced).
  \item \textbf{Clarity OK} --- the conclusion is readable to a smart
        outsider.
  \item \textbf{No leakage OK} --- the conclusion does not expose
        private uploads or quoted material from sources that aren't
        public.
\end{itemize}

Approving the item mints a permanent public version (with a slug and
a version number --- republishing bumps the version) and the
workshop's ledger writes a cryptographic signature attached to the
canonical hash of the payload. The Codex stores the signature; the
signing keys never live inside the website.

\screenshot{06_publication_queue.png}{The publication review queue. Each row shows the conclusion summary, its source uploads, its adversarial status, the checklist state, and the approve / send-back / decline controls.}{op-pub}

This is the only path to the public site for a conclusion. If you, as
a general founder, want your work to be considered for publication,
you do it by pushing the conclusion forward in \page{Conclusions}; the
operator then picks it up from the queue.

\section{The methodology review week}

Once a quarter, the firm runs a five-day exercise where founder-alpha
walks the firm's recent work and writes a structured account of what
the firm learned. Each day of the week:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item The workshop produces a draft body that summarizes the day's
        focus (e.g., ``Monday: principles accepted this quarter and
        their domains of applicability'').
  \item Founder-alpha edits the body and writes the final account.
  \item When founder-alpha signs the day cryptographically, the row
        becomes immutable. To revise after signing, founder-alpha
        must clear the signature first --- which is itself an audited
        event.
\end{itemize}

The history page shows every prior week's signed daily summaries.
This is the firm's durable record of methodological self-assessment.
As a general founder, you can read it; you cannot author it.

\section{Source standing and source triage}

When an outside source the firm has cited changes its public status
(retraction, correction, dispute, expiration), the workshop fires a
source-standing event. The standing change cascades into one triage
row per affected conclusion, which lands on \page{Source triage}.

For each row founder-alpha:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \texttt{confirm} --- the source change applies; the cited
        conclusion needs revision.
  \item \texttt{override} --- the operator believes the source change
        does not affect the conclusion. The override is logged.
  \item \texttt{dismiss} --- the row is closed without action.
\end{itemize}

A separate trigger for the same surface is a citation-verdict --- an
NLI judge has decided that the way the firm cited a source
(\textsc{supports}, \textsc{contradicts}, \textsc{qualifies},
\textsc{mentions}) does not match what the source actually says.
Mismatches block publication of new conclusions that depend on the
affected citation.

\section{The unified attention queue}

\page{Attention} is one page that pulls together everything the
system thinks an operator should look at: principles awaiting triage,
drift events, contradictions, source-triage rows, decay items,
publication-review needs-revision rows, methodology-review-week days,
and unanswered critiques.

Each row supports \texttt{snooze}, \texttt{dismiss}, and
\texttt{unsnooze}. The actions are append-only so the queue's history
is itself auditable.

As a general founder you will see attention items on your dashboard
when they affect material you produced; the unified operator queue
is operator-only.

\section{Deletion vs retraction}

Two adjacent flows; understand the difference because both can affect
material you've worked on.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{Deletion} --- removal of source material. Soft-deletes
        the upload; all derived bridge rows are hard-deleted, and any
        conclusion left with zero sources is then hard-deleted too.
        The public surface stops resolving the affected rows. Used
        for: user / subject deletion requests; legal or compliance
        reasons. Performed by founder-alpha on the admin surface.
  \item \textbf{Retraction} --- removal of a \emph{published} output.
        The retraction is itself a published artifact: it creates a
        new public version that retracts the previous one, leaving an
        audit trail. Used for: an article or conclusion the firm no
        longer endorses. Performed by founder-alpha on the
        publication surface.
\end{itemize}

A retraction is a stance. A deletion is a removal of evidence. The
firm makes the distinction visible because deleting evidence quietly
is the easiest way for an audit trail to become useless.

\section{Roles and the founders roster}

The firm has three roles: \texttt{admin} (the founder-alpha
operators), \texttt{founder} (general founders --- everyone reading
this), and \texttt{viewer} (read-only).

\textbf{Founders are never hard-deleted.} If a founder leaves the
firm, their row stays, with all relations preserved, so that revision
events, attributed conclusions, and public-reply consent records
remain intact for audit.

Signup is intentionally not exposed. New founders are seeded by
administrators. There is no ``sign up'' button anywhere on the public
site.

\section{Credentials and machine-to-machine keys}

Machine-to-machine credentials --- the kind the desktop apps use to
talk to the Codex on behalf of a founder --- are managed centrally.
When you install Dialectic and sign in with your firm email and
password, Dialectic gets a credential back from the Codex through a
dedicated app-login endpoint. You never see or paste a key.

API keys for third-party integrations (CLIs, internal scripts, etc.)
are minted by founder-alpha on the operator surface, with each key
shown once in plaintext at mint time and the rest of the time stored
only as a bcrypt hash. Revoking a key is also an operator action.

This is why the platform doesn't have a ``mint your own API key''
flow for general founders: the firm wants a single accountable place
where machine credentials are issued and revoked.

\section{Kill switches}

There is no global kill switch. There are several precise ones, and
that is deliberate. Each disables one specific risky surface:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{Currents ingestion off.} The next scheduler cycle
        writes no new events. Existing opinions stay up.
  \item \textbf{Articles dispatcher off.} Existing articles stay up;
        no new ones are clustered or written.
  \item \textbf{Master live-trading off.} The platform-wide live-bet
        flag. This is gate 5 of the eight-gate contract (Guide 5).
        Setting it off refuses every downstream submit.
  \item \textbf{Per-submit kill switch.} Gate 8. Re-checked
        immediately before each order placement; trips even bets that
        already passed operator confirmation.
\end{itemize}

A general founder may notice the consequences of any of these (no
new Currents opinions; no new bets) on the dashboard. The toggles
themselves are operator-only.

\section{Live authorization, revoked}

The inverse of gates 6--7 in Guide 5. Founder-alpha can:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{Revoke per-prediction authorization.} The row drops
        out of the public grid.
  \item \textbf{Cancel an open order before fill.} For exchanges that
        permit cancel, the operator UI exposes a cancel button on
        the position row.
  \item \textbf{Close an existing position.} The portfolio view has a
        manual close action. This is a real-funds action; it is gated
        behind the same confirmation modal as opening a bet.
\end{itemize}

\section{Rigor gate, peer review, adversarial}

These are the publishing-readiness gates that sit just before the
publication review queue:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \page{Rigor gate} --- the per-publication audit that enforces
        the firm's quantitative-formalisation contract: every approved
        principle-shaped conclusion must declare a null hypothesis,
        at least one metric, and at least one test. The gate refuses
        publication if any of these is missing.
  \item \page{Peer review} --- the queue where founders cross-review
        each other's conclusions before they enter the publication
        queue. Peer review is the only operator-adjacent surface
        general founders contribute to directly: if founder-alpha
        asks you to peer-review a colleague's conclusion, this is
        where you do it.
  \item \page{Adversarial} --- the founder-side view of the
        adversarial cycle's output. An operator can apply a human
        override when the system's verdict needs correction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Outreach drafts}

\page{Social} contains the operator-drafted outreach threads that go
out to external replicators, journalists, and collaborators. The page
generates a draft from the firm's recent public material; the
operator edits and either posts manually or exports for sending
through a separate channel.

\begin{preview}
Automated sending is not enabled. Today, the outreach surface is a
draft generator plus a send-when-I-say-so review queue. The Codex
never directly posts.
\end{preview}

\section{Scoreboard, eval, post-mortem}

Three closely related surfaces that record how the firm grades itself:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \page{Scoreboard} --- the firm's calibration performance, the
        same numbers that drive the public calibration manifest, but
        broken down by method, by domain, and by reviewer.
  \item \page{Eval} and per-run pages --- the firm's evaluation runs
        against external batteries (e.g., forecasting tournaments,
        replication-public sets). Each run carries its corpus bundle,
        the predictions produced, the resolutions observed, Brier
        and log-loss, expected calibration error, and coverage.
  \item \page{Post-Mortem} --- the post-mortem ledger: when the firm
        made a mistake, what the mistake was, what the cause was, and
        what was changed.
\end{itemize}

These are operator surfaces only in the sense that founder-alpha
authors entries on them. As a general founder, you can read them, and
in some cases your work will be cited as part of the audit.

\section{Provenance and cascade}

\page{Provenance} is the global view of the firm's audit graph: which
method invocation produced which artifact, which artifact depends on
which other artifact, which external sources fed the chain.

\page{Cascade} (\route{/cascade/<conclusionId>}) is the per-conclusion
impact-analysis view (covered in Guide 2 from the founder side).

\section{The reason for all this ceremony}

It is fair to ask why the firm builds in so many gates, queues, and
signatures when most days nothing goes wrong. The answer is the same
answer that animates the whole platform: \emph{Theseus's product is
its recorded reasoning, not its opinions}.

That product is only worth anything if a later reviewer can inspect
the record and trust what they see. Publication review, methodology
signing, source-standing triage, deletion-vs-retraction, kill
switches, live-bet gates --- all of it exists so the record is
trustworthy when it matters most. The work of founder-alpha is
mostly invisible; what's left behind is what makes the firm credible
when something does go wrong.

\section{The one-page checklist when something is wrong}

(For founder-alpha's reference, in the unlucky event you need to do
this yourself.)

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item Look at the dashboard. Is the operations card red? What does
        the red text say?
  \item Run the relevant non-writing diagnostic before flipping any
        switch.
  \item If a kill switch is the right answer, flip the most specific
        one. Do not disable broader surfaces than you need to.
  \item Restart the affected service with the documented helper
        script; do not stop the entire stack.
  \item Once stable, file a critique so the failure has a written
        record. The post-mortem ledger picks it up.
\end{enumerate}
