\begin{tldr}
Theseus is a small group of web surfaces that together turn the
documents and conversations you upload into a coherent, citation-grounded
view of what your firm believes. This guide is a thirty-minute first
session: you will sign in, upload one document, watch claims extract,
ask the Oracle a question, and observe a Currents opinion form. By the
end you will know which screen does which thing, and which of the five
other guides to read next.
\end{tldr}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em,topsep=0.2em]

\section{What Theseus is}

Theseus is not one product; it is a small constellation of related
surfaces that share a database. Three of them matter for the first
session:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \page{Codex} (authed, founder-facing). The control plane. You
        upload documents, inspect extracted claims, ask the Oracle,
        and manage published material here.
  \item \page{Public site} (reader-facing). What outside readers see:
        articles, conclusions, principles, Currents opinions, the
        forecasts grid.
  \item \page{Currents} (public, separate origin). Live commentary on
        outside events the firm's corpus has something to say about.
\end{itemize}

The Codex is what you spend most of your time in. The public site is
what your work eventually surfaces on. Currents is a constant
background process you read like a feed.

\section{The first thirty minutes}

\subsection{Step 1 --- sign in and orient}

Open the Codex deployment. You land on the dashboard. The left rail
shows the top-level surfaces; for now you only need four:
\page{Upload}, \page{Knowledge}, \page{Ask}, \page{Currents}.

\screenshot{01_quick_start_dashboard.png}{The authenticated dashboard, with the four surfaces this guide uses highlighted.}{qs-dashboard}

\subsection{Step 2 --- upload one document}

Click \page{Upload}. Drag a file in. PDFs, markdown, text files, and
transcripts are all accepted. After the upload completes the file
appears in the upload list with a small \texttt{processing} badge.
This badge clears once extraction finishes, which usually takes a
few seconds for short text and a minute or two for a long PDF.

You do not need to do anything else here. The pipeline takes the file
from this point. See Guide 2 for what it does to the file along the
way.

\subsection{Step 3 --- watch claims extract}

Open \page{Knowledge}. You will see the upload you just made, with a
small \texttt{claims} count growing as extraction runs. Click into
your upload to see the extracted claims --- short, declarative
statements with provenance back to the source text.

If your upload is the first piece of content in the corpus, this list
is also the first row of your firm's working memory.

\subsection{Step 4 --- ask the Oracle}

Open \page{Ask}. Type a question that the document you just uploaded
would plausibly inform. The Oracle answers with inline citations back
to claims and uploaded content. Click any citation to jump to the
source.

For first-session expectations: do not expect the Oracle to be all-
knowing. It only knows what is in the corpus. With one document
uploaded, its answers will be narrow. That is correct behavior.

\screenshot{01_quick_start_ask.png}{An Oracle answer, with inline citations linking back to the uploaded source.}{qs-ask}

If the Oracle returns a hallucination warning or a low-confidence
banner, do not be alarmed. Read Guide 3 for what those mean and what
to do.

\subsection{Step 5 --- observe a Currents opinion}

Open \page{Currents}. If the Currents pipeline has been live for any
period, you will see public opinions written by Theseus on outside
events. Each opinion has a small audit trail of citations back to firm
material. If the corpus is too sparse to support commentary on a
particular event, you will see an explicit abstention rather than
fabricated commentary.

Currents is the place to develop a feel for the firm's voice in the
wild. Guide 4 explains where opinions come from, what controls them,
and how to read their audit trails.

\section{What you just saw}

Five surfaces, one pipeline. Documents enter through \page{Upload},
get broken into claims that live in \page{Knowledge}, get clustered
into principles that surface on the public site, get cited by the
\page{Oracle} when you ask questions, and get cited by Currents when
the firm has something to say about outside news. Everything else in
the Codex is a refinement of one of these five surfaces.

\section{Common first-session frictions}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{The Oracle returned ``I don't have enough sources.''}
        That is the correct response when the corpus is sparse. Upload
        more material in the same topic area, or rephrase the question
        to something your single uploaded document actually addresses.
  \item \textbf{No Currents opinions are appearing.} Currents requires
        an X ingestion token and at least three relevant Conclusions
        in the corpus. See \file{docs/operator/CURRENTS.md} for the
        operator-side health check, and Guide 4 for the pipeline.
  \item \textbf{Upload sits in \texttt{processing} for a long time.}
        Large PDFs take time. If processing exceeds five minutes for
        a single document, check the founder dashboard's
        \page{OperatorPulse} card --- it shows which scheduler loops
        are live.
\end{itemize}
