\begin{tldr}
Everything the firm reads, hears, or records eventually becomes a
\emph{claim} (one sentence, one speaker), gets grouped with similar
claims into a \emph{principle} (a durable belief), and earns a
\emph{conclusion} (a vetted, principle-shaped position) when the
workshop is willing to commit. This guide walks the full corpus side
of the Codex: the surfaces (\page{Library}, \page{Upload},
\page{Transcripts}, \page{Knowledge}, \page{Conclusions},
\page{Contradictions}, \page{Open Questions}, \page{Explorer},
\page{Knowledge graph}, \page{Principles}, \page{Methods}), what each
is for, and the small set of routine actions you take to keep the
firm's working memory clean. Approval, deletion, and retraction of
public material is operator-only and is covered in Guide 6.
\end{tldr}

\begin{youwillneed}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em,topsep=0.2em]
  \item A signed-in Codex session.
  \item At least one ingested upload --- if you do not have one,
        complete Guide 1 first.
  \item Twenty to thirty minutes the first time through; about ten
        minutes a week for routine triage afterwards.
\end{itemize}
\end{youwillneed}

Two passes happen on every ingested upload. The top row distills
durable \emph{principles} from clustered claims. The bottom row builds
\emph{conclusions} --- principle-shaped, source-grounded positions
that the firm is willing to commit to, accompanied by a methodology
profile (how this source reasoned) and adversarial objections (what
the strongest case against this conclusion looks like).

\section{The Library --- everything the firm has uploaded}

\page{Library} (\route{/library}) is the org-wide inventory of every
upload. Rows include written documents, podcast transcripts, Dialectic
session files, annotation bundles, and external material. Each row
shows the title, the source type, the founder who uploaded it, the
visibility (\texttt{org}, \texttt{semi-private}, or \texttt{private}),
and the processing state.

You can delete your own uploads from this page; the file goes through
a short ``please confirm'' step and is then soft-deleted. If you need
something somebody else uploaded removed, the library lets you open a
\page{Deletion request} that the owner accepts or declines. (Outright
hard deletion of material the firm has already cited publicly is
operator-only and goes through a different surface --- see Guide 6.)

\section{Upload --- raw material in}

\page{Upload} (\route{/upload}) is the form. Drop a file in, pick a
source type, pick a visibility, optionally check ``publish as a blog
post.''

The form accepts text-like files (\texttt{.md}, \texttt{.txt},
\texttt{.vtt}, \texttt{.jsonl}, \texttt{.json}), PDFs, \texttt{.docx},
and audio (\texttt{.mp3}, \texttt{.m4a}, \texttt{.wav}, \texttt{.webm},
\texttt{.ogg}). For very large files, the Codex automatically uses a
direct-to-storage upload flow behind the scenes.

After submit, the workshop runs in the background. The detail page for
your upload streams the workshop's progress live --- you can refresh
and see fresh lines. When the workshop finishes, the badge clears, the
upload's status becomes \texttt{ingested}, and counts appear next to
the row: how many claims were extracted, how many clusters were
produced, how many methodology profiles were written.

\section{Transcripts --- reading the source}

For uploads with a transcript shape (podcasts, Dialectic sessions, VTT
files), \page{Transcripts} (\route{/transcripts/<uploadId>}) presents
the text in chunks, with speaker labels and millisecond timestamps
where those are available. This is the source-of-truth view: every
claim elsewhere in the system carries a pointer that lands here, on
the exact span it came from.

\section{The atomic claim}

A claim is one sentence, one speaker, one assertion, plus a fingerprint
of its meaning. The workshop refuses to roll claims into paragraphs ---
the whole point is to be able to reason about them individually.

What a claim carries:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item The text.
  \item Who said it (speaker label, founder identity if known).
  \item Where it came from (upload, source span start and end).
  \item Its disciplines (one or more, from a fixed vocabulary).
  \item Its fingerprint (an embedding vector).
  \item A type --- \texttt{factual}, \texttt{methodological},
        \texttt{normative}, \texttt{predictive}, \texttt{definitional},
        or \texttt{interpretive}.
  \item Its origin --- \texttt{founder} (the speaker's own assertion)
        or \texttt{external} (a quoted external position; the workshop
        strips these out of inference).
  \item Hedges and evidence pointers.
\end{itemize}

The workshop runs a guard on input that strips out interview prompts
and quoted external positions \emph{before} segmentation, so ``what do
you think of X?'' (the host asking) doesn't end up as the founder
asserting X.

\section{Knowledge --- claims, browsable}

\page{Knowledge} (\route{/knowledge}) is the claim browser. Filter by
upload, by speaker, by discipline, by claim type. Click any claim to
see its source span, its embedding neighbors, and the conclusions that
cite it.

\section{Conclusions --- the firm's vetted positions}

\page{Conclusions} (\route{/conclusions}) is the most important
surface on the corpus side. A conclusion is a principle-shaped,
source-grounded statement of something the firm is willing to commit
to.

Every conclusion carries:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item Its text and rationale.
  \item A \textbf{confidence tier} on a ladder: \texttt{firm} (most
        confident), \texttt{founder} (a founder vouches), \texttt{open}
        (under review), or one of the demoted tiers
        (\texttt{moderate}, \texttt{low}, \texttt{speculative}) that
        the adversarial cycle can drop a conclusion into.
  \item Its supporting principles, its evidence chain (the underlying
        claims), and any dissenting claims.
  \item Its source uploads.
  \item Its \textbf{methodology profile} --- a separate record of
        \emph{how} the source reasoned (see below).
  \item Its \textbf{quality score} (MQS) on the five working criteria
        (progressivity, severity, aim-method fit, compressibility,
        domain sensitivity).
  \item Its \textbf{principle shape} contract (next section).
\end{itemize}

\section{The principle-shape contract}

The firm requires every principle-grade conclusion to declare what
kind of thing it is and where it applies. The contract has five
mandatory fields:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \texttt{principleKind} --- one of seven shapes: \texttt{RULE}
        (a normative ``do X''), \texttt{CRITERION} (``X is the test
        for Y''), \texttt{MECHANISM} (``X causes Y by Z''),
        \texttt{HEURISTIC} (``X usually works in case Y''),
        \texttt{DEFINITION}, \texttt{FORMULA}, or \texttt{ALGORITHM}.
  \item \texttt{domainOfApplicability} --- a 300-character description
        of \emph{where this is meant to hold and where it stops
        working}. Required. ``Always'' is not acceptable.
  \item \texttt{quantifiableProxies} --- up to five measurable proxies
        that would let an outside reviewer falsify the principle.
  \item \texttt{decisionExamples} --- up to three concrete decisions
        the principle would direct.
  \item \texttt{sourceSpan} --- a verbatim substring of a source chunk
        that the principle is anchored to. If the quoted span doesn't
        literally appear in the source, the workshop refuses to write
        the row.
\end{itemize}

This is the firm's standing answer to ``where would this stop being
true?'' A conclusion without a domain of applicability cannot
graduate to \texttt{firm} tier.

\section{Quantitative Formalisation --- the falsifiability layer}

For any conclusion that is being prepared for publication, the
quantitative formalisation page (\route{/principles/<id>/quantitative})
shows whether a falsifiability layer has been attached: a null
hypothesis, one or more metrics, one or more statistical tests, and
one or more data sources. The workshop refuses to mark a quantitative
formalisation \texttt{APPROVED} without all four.

This is the operational form of the working criterion ``the test
should be severe enough that the claim would have failed if it were
wrong.'' If you cannot state what would falsify the principle, the
principle does not get to be public.

\section{Contradictions and Open Questions}

\page{Contradictions} (\route{/contradictions}) lists active
claim-vs-claim disagreements the workshop has flagged. Each row
carries: claim A, claim B, the severity score, a six-layer breakdown
of how the coherence engine voted, a short narrative, and a status
(\texttt{active}, \texttt{resolved}, or \texttt{dismissed}).

\page{Open Questions} (\route{/open-questions}) is the unresolved bin:
pairs the six layers couldn't reach a majority on. These are good
candidates for the next conversation to revisit.

Resolve or dismiss either kind from its detail page, attaching a brief
note. The note is part of the audit trail.

\section{Explorer --- the embedding map}

\page{Explorer} (\route{/explorer}) is a 3D map of the firm's
conclusions in embedding space. Each conclusion is a point; similar
conclusions cluster together. The view supports:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item Saved camera positions encoded in the URL, so you can share a
        view by copy-pasting the link.
  \item Hotkeys for toggling labels, axes, and confidence tiers.
  \item Empty-state diagnostics --- if the embedding index isn't built
        for some conclusions, the panel tells you which ones.
\end{itemize}

\screenshot{02_explorer.png}{The Explorer view, with conclusions as points and confidence tiers color-coded.}{ex-3d}

A second, graph-shaped view lives at \page{Knowledge graph}
(\route{/knowledge}, the graph tab) and shows principles as nodes with
typed edges (\textsc{supports}, \textsc{contradicts}, \textsc{refines},
\textsc{instantiates}, and others).

\section{The principle triage queue}

\page{Principles} (\route{/principles/queue}) is the queue of
candidate principles the workshop has distilled but not yet committed
to. Three columns:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{New drafts.} Just produced. Read the principle text,
        scan supporting claims, scan objections. Decide: \texttt{accept},
        \texttt{merge into existing}, or \texttt{reject with reason}.
  \item \textbf{Needs re-review.} Either contradicted by a newer
        cluster or flagged by the adversarial pass. The previous
        decision should be re-justified or rolled back.
  \item \textbf{Accepted.} The firm's working memory. Editable in
        place. Whether an accepted principle is also \emph{publicly
        visible} is a separate operator-only step.
\end{itemize}

\page{Principles} $\to$ \page{Triage}
(\route{/principles/<id>/triage}) is where you commit a decision. The
reason you give for a rejection is read by the next distillation pass,
which uses it to avoid re-proposing the same cluster.

\section{Methodology profiles --- how a source reasons}

\page{Methods} (\route{/methods}) is the firm's registry of reasoning
patterns: \texttt{first\_principles\_decomposition},
\texttt{adversarial\_revision}, \texttt{analogical\_transfer},
\texttt{dialogic\_unfolding}, and others. Each method has a one-
sentence rule, a transfer condition (when to use it), an assumption
it presumes, and a known failure mode.

For every upload, the workshop writes a \emph{methodology profile}: a
short record of \emph{how} this piece of material reasoned, with four
verbatim anchor quotes pinned to sentence indices in the source. The
profile carries a confidence score, the inferred transfer targets, and
a dedupe key so re-running over the same material updates rather than
duplicates.

\page{Methods} $\to$ \texttt{[name]/[version]} surfaces a per-method
detail page that includes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item A track record: per-domain Brier score, calibration slope with
        bootstrap CI, and the severity-pass rate.
  \item A failures table.
  \item A domain bound (the centroids that define the method's domain
        of applicability in embedding space).
  \item A changelog (every version of the method source).
\end{itemize}

A method can be \texttt{active}, \texttt{under\_review},
\texttt{deprecated}, or \texttt{retired}. \page{Methods} $\to$
\page{Candidates} (\route{/methods/candidates}) is the inbox of
proposed new methods; the \page{Methods graph}
(\route{/methods/graph}) shows their dependency structure.

\section{Source standing --- when a citation's status changes}

The firm's published material cites outside sources. Those sources can
change status over time --- a paper gets retracted, a study is
corrected, an article is disputed, an article expires behind a
paywall. The workshop tracks these as \emph{source-standing} events.
When a standing flips, every conclusion that cited the affected source
is flagged for review.

You will see the resulting attention items on your dashboard. The
review action itself --- confirming, overriding, or dismissing the
flag --- is operator-only and is covered in Guide 6. For most
founders, the impact of source-standing changes shows up as
conclusions that quietly get an updated status banner.

\section{Cascade --- what would change if I revised this?}

\page{Cascade} (\route{/cascade/<conclusionId>}) is the impact-analysis
view. Pick a conclusion; the page shows every downstream artifact
whose production graph touches it: which methods produced what, which
methodology profiles depend on it, which Currents opinions and
Forecasts cite it. This is what makes the firm's ``reanalyze without
wiping'' rule operational: when a method version bumps, the cascade is
the precise set of things to revisit.

\section{Decay --- staleness}

\page{Decay} (\route{/decay}) lists the firm's knowledge that is aging
or stale. A conclusion ages by a fixed schedule or by trigger:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item \textbf{Fixed interval} --- e.g., revisit annually.
  \item \textbf{Evidence changed} --- a source the conclusion depends
        on was retracted or its standing changed.
  \item \textbf{Method version bump} --- the method that produced the
        conclusion has a new version.
  \item \textbf{Embedding drift} --- the conclusion's meaning has
        shifted beyond a threshold relative to its origin snapshot.
  \item \textbf{Outcome observed} --- a forecast or prediction the
        conclusion implied has resolved.
  \item \textbf{Calibration regression} --- the method's per-domain
        track record has worsened beyond a threshold.
\end{itemize}

Each row offers a revalidate, retire, or update action. Revalidating
re-runs the relevant workshop passes; retiring marks the conclusion
\texttt{retired} but keeps the row for audit; updating creates a new
version.

\section{The founder's weekly rhythm on the corpus side}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{Daily glance.} \page{Dashboard}. See active principles,
        recent conclusions, drift events, operations health.
  \item \textbf{15 minutes weekly.} \page{Principles} triage queue.
        Accept, merge, or reject the drafts that have accumulated.
  \item \textbf{10 minutes weekly.} \page{Contradictions} and
        \page{Open Questions}. Resolve or dismiss with a note.
  \item \textbf{As needed.} \page{Decay} when something aged; the
        attention items on the dashboard when they appear.
\end{itemize}

If you ignore triage for a week, drafts accumulate but nothing
collapses --- the firm's automatic answers just keep working off the
last batch of accepted principles. The corpus stops evolving, not the
system.

\section{Common failure modes}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \textbf{Drafts pile up.} Reserve a recurring slot for triage.
        Five to ten minutes per draft is realistic.
  \item \textbf{The cluster is junk.} Reject the draft with a reason.
        The reason feeds back into the next distillation pass.
  \item \textbf{A conclusion was demoted by the adversarial cycle.}
        Read the objection. Decide whether the firm has a counter
        you'd like to surface, or whether the demotion is just.
  \item \textbf{A source you depended on got retracted.} The system
        already filed an attention item; founder-alpha will work
        through it on the operator side.
\end{itemize}
