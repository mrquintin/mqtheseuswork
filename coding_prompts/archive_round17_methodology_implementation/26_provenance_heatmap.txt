Goal: a provenance heatmap on every public article. Each sentence is
shaded by the cascade weight of its supporting evidence. Strong-evidence
sentences read normally; weak-evidence sentences are visibly weak. This
turns the article into an inspectable artifact — readers can see at a
glance which claims rest on what.

Before you start — read:
1. `theseus-codex/src/components/ConclusionView.tsx` — current article
   rendering.
2. `theseus-codex/src/components/CitationPopover.tsx`.
3. `noosphere/noosphere/cascade/graph.py` — cascade weights.
4. The lineage work from prompt 17.
5. The source-credibility work from prompt 19.

Implement:

A. Per-sentence evidence assembler. For each sentence in a published
   article, walk the cascade graph from the sentence's anchored
   conclusion(s) to the supporting sources, multiply edge weights ×
   source credibility, sum and normalize. Output: a `provenance` ∈
   [0,1] per sentence.

B. Visual treatment. Render shading as a faint background tint along
   the gutter, NOT inline highlighting (which fights the typography).
   A toggle ("Show provenance") in the article toolbar reveals/hides
   the gutter. Default off; a one-time tooltip on first article view
   teaches the reader.

C. Sentence-level pop-over. Clicking a low-provenance sentence opens
   a small panel listing every supporting source and edge with the
   cascade weights, source credibility, and citation verdict (prompt
   20).

D. Accessibility. The shading does NOT carry information that is not
   also available textually. The panel is keyboard-reachable. A
   screen reader can request "provenance summary for this sentence."

E. Performance. The heatmap data ships with the article HTML at
   render time, not on hover. A 3,000-word article must add no more
   than 25KB gzipped of provenance data.

F. Tests. A snapshot test that the heatmap data round-trips through
   a published article view; an a11y test that the shading is not
   the only signal; a perf test on a synthetic 5,000-word article.

Constraints
- The shading does not let a reader infer the identity of private
  sources. If a sentence rests partially on a private source, the
  provenance number includes it (so the firm is honest about its
  evidence base) but the panel hides the private source's identity.
- The toggle state persists per visitor (cookie/localStorage). If
  storage is restricted, default to off.
