# The Theseus Curriculum: Reading Lists, Research Projects, and Resources for Meta-Methodological Thinking

This document is designed for each founder to develop their own conception of second-order (and third-order) thinking — the study of HOW to determine truth, rather than WHAT is true. The readings are organized by depth and domain. The research projects are open-ended investigations each founder can pursue independently.

---

## PART I: CORE READING LIST

These are the foundational texts. Every founder should engage with these. They are ordered not by difficulty but by the logical dependencies between ideas.

### Tier 1: The Problem of Method (Start Here)

1. **René Descartes — *Discourse on the Method* (1637)**
   Not the Meditations (which are first-order: "I think therefore I am"). The Discourse is the meta-text: Descartes asking "what rules should govern inquiry itself?" His four rules (accept nothing without evidence, divide problems, build from simple to complex, enumerate completely) are the first formal attempt at meta-methodology in the modern tradition. ~60 pages. Read it in an afternoon.

2. **George Pólya — *How to Solve It* (1945)**
   The finest book ever written on heuristic method. Pólya doesn't tell you math answers; he tells you how to find math answers. His "heuristic catalog" (analogy, generalization, specialization, variation, decomposition) is a meta-methodology for problem-solving. Applicable far beyond mathematics. ~250 pages.

3. **Karl Popper — *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934/1959)**
   Popper's actual argument is not "falsify things" (a caricature). His real contribution is meta-methodological: a theory of what *demarcates* science from non-science. The demarcation criterion (falsifiability) is a criterion for evaluating METHODS, not claims. Read especially Chapters 1-4 and the appendices on degrees of corroboration. ~480 pages, dense.

4. **Thomas Kuhn — *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962)**
   The most important counter-argument to Popper. Kuhn argues that scientists don't actually follow Popper's method — they work within paradigms that determine what counts as a problem, what counts as a solution, and what counts as evidence. The implications for meta-methodology are profound: methods are not paradigm-independent; they exist within frameworks. ~210 pages.

### Tier 2: Meta-Methodology Proper

5. **Imre Lakatos — *The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes* (1978)**
   The synthesis of Popper and Kuhn. Lakatos introduces the key meta-methodological distinction: progressive vs. degenerating research programmes. A programme is progressive if its methodology generates novel predictions; degenerating if it only produces ad hoc defenses. This is the most directly operationalizable framework for Theseus. ~200 pages.

6. **Larry Laudan — *Progress and Its Problems* (1977)**
   Laudan's central insight: the rationality of a method depends on its problem-solving effectiveness, not its truth-tracking. And methods, theories, and aims form a reticulated network — no single element is foundational. This challenges the assumption that truth is the only aim of inquiry and opens up a richer meta-methodology. ~260 pages.

7. **Deborah Mayo — *Statistical Inference as Severe Testing* (2018)**
   The most rigorous modern framework for evaluating evidential methods. Mayo's "severity principle" — evidence supports a claim only if the test would very probably have detected the claim's falsity had it been false — is the sharpest tool in the meta-methodological toolkit. Technical in places but the core argument is accessible. ~486 pages.

8. **Paul Feyerabend — *Against Method* (1975)**
   The strongest possible counter-argument to the entire project of meta-methodology. Feyerabend argues that no methodological rule has been universally followed in the history of science, and that methodological anarchism ("anything goes") better describes actual scientific progress. You must wrestle with this honestly. If Feyerabend is right, the meta-methodological project needs fundamental revision. ~336 pages.

### Tier 3: Formal Foundations

9. **E.T. Jaynes — *Probability Theory: The Logic of Science* (2003)**
   The most rigorous exposition of Bayesian reasoning as an extension of formal logic. Jaynes shows that probability theory is not a branch of mathematics applied to uncertainty — it IS the logic of reasoning under uncertainty. This is foundational for any computational meta-methodology. ~727 pages, mathematical.

10. **Judea Pearl — *Causality* (2009, 2nd ed.)**
    Pearl's do-calculus is a formal meta-methodology for causal inference. It doesn't tell you what causes what; it tells you what you need to observe to determine what causes what. The distinction between seeing (correlation) and doing (intervention) is a methodological distinction, not a substantive one. ~484 pages, technical.

11. **David Deutsch — *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011)**
    Deutsch's argument that all progress comes from good explanations — and that "good explanation" can be formally characterized (hard to vary). His epistemology connects Popper, computation theory, and physics into a unified theory of what makes knowledge possible. ~487 pages.

12. **Ming Li & Paul Vitányi — *An Introduction to Kolmogorov Complexity and Its Applications* (2008, 3rd ed.)**
    The mathematical formalization of "simplicity." Kolmogorov complexity is the theoretical foundation for Solomonoff induction — the provably optimal inference procedure. Understanding this is understanding the mathematical north star of all methodology. ~790 pages, highly technical.

### Tier 4: Adjacent Wisdom

13. **Douglas Hofstadter — *Gödel, Escher, Bach* (1979)**
    On self-reference, strange loops, and the limits of formal systems. Directly relevant to the question of whether a meta-methodology can evaluate itself (it can, as a fixed point). ~777 pages.

14. **Nassim Taleb — *The Black Swan* (2007) + *Antifragile* (2012)**
    Taleb's contribution is methodological, not predictive. His key insight: most methodologies are fragile — they work until they don't, catastrophically. A robust meta-methodology must account for tail risks, domain dependence, and the difference between Mediocristan and Extremistan. ~700 pages total.

15. **C.S. Peirce — Selected writings on pragmatism and inquiry**
    Peirce is arguably the deepest meta-methodologist in the American tradition. His theory of inquiry as self-correcting, his classification of reasoning (deduction, induction, abduction), and his pragmatic maxim (the meaning of a concept is its practical consequences) are all meta-methodological contributions. Start with "The Fixation of Belief" (1877) and "How to Make Our Ideas Clear" (1878). ~50 pages for these two essays.

16. **Michael Polanyi — *Personal Knowledge* (1958)**
    The strongest argument that not all methodology can be made explicit. Polanyi's concept of "tacit knowledge" — knowing more than we can tell — challenges the assumption that meta-methodology can be fully formalized. If Polanyi is right, there are limits to what Theseus can encode in software, and those limits must be acknowledged. ~428 pages.

## PART II: DOMAIN-SPECIFIC READING

### For the Investing Faculty

- **Howard Marks — *The Most Important Thing* (2011)** — "Second-level thinking" is a meta-methodological concept: thinking about how others think, not just about what's true.
- **George Soros — *The Alchemy of Finance* (1987)** — Reflexivity as a methodological challenge: the method changes the subject it studies.
- **Philip Tetlock — *Superforecasting* (2015)** — Empirical study of what makes forecasting methodology effective. The "foxes vs. hedgehogs" distinction is meta-methodological.
- **Annie Duke — *Thinking in Bets* (2018)** — Decision-making as probabilistic reasoning. The "resulting" fallacy (judging methodology by outcomes) is a meta-methodological error.
- **Ben Graham — *The Intelligent Investor* (1949), Chapter 20** — The "margin of safety" is a methodological concept, not a valuation tool. It's about building error tolerance into your method.

### For the Media / Writing Faculty

- **Richard Feynman — *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!* (1985)** — Feynman's "cargo cult science" lecture is the most accessible meta-methodological critique ever delivered. What distinguishes real science from the appearance of science.
- **Daniel Kahneman — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011)** — Catalogue of systematic methodological failures built into human cognition.
- **Jonathan Rauch — *The Constitution of Knowledge* (2021)** — How institutions encode truth-finding methodology. The "reality-based community" as a methodological commitment.
- **Eliezer Yudkowsky — *Rationality: From AI to Zombies* (2015)** — Extensive treatment of epistemic rationality as methodology. Free online at lesswrong.com.

### For the Research Faculty

- **Andrew Gelman & Jennifer Hill — *Data Analysis Using Regression and Multilevel/Hierarchical Models* (2006)** — The gold standard for applied statistical methodology.
- **John Ioannidis — "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" (2005)** — The most-cited paper on methodological failure in science. Essential reading.
- **Jacob Cohen — "The Earth Is Round (p < .05)" (1994)** — Classic critique of null hypothesis significance testing as a methodology.
- **Ronald Fisher vs. Neyman-Pearson debate** — The foundational dispute about what statistical inference IS. Understanding both sides is understanding the meta-methodological problem in statistics.

### For the Philosophy of Mathematics / Formal Methods

- **Saunders Mac Lane — *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971)** — The standard text on category theory, which is meta-mathematics: the study of relationships between mathematical structures.
- **Eugenia Cheng — *The Joy of Abstraction* (2022)** — Accessible introduction to category theory for non-specialists.
- **Homotopy Type Theory: Univalent Foundations of Mathematics (2013)** — Free online. The most radical recent attempt to rebuild the foundations of mathematics. Deeply meta-methodological.

## PART III: RESEARCH PROJECTS FOR THE FOUNDERS

These are open-ended investigations. Each founder should pick 1-2 and pursue them over 3-6 months. The goal is not to reach conclusions but to develop independent conceptions of meta-methodology.

### Project 1: The Progressivity Audit

**Question:** Is Theseus's research programme progressive or degenerating in Lakatos's sense?

**Method:** Catalogue every prediction the firm has made (explicitly or implicitly) over the last year. For each prediction, determine: (a) Was it novel (not just an explanation of known facts)? (b) Has it been tested? (c) Was it corroborated or falsified? Compute the ratio of novel corroborated predictions to total predictions. If the ratio is declining, the programme is degenerating.

**Output:** A written report answering: "By Lakatos's criteria, is Theseus's methodology getting better or worse? What would reverse the trend?"

### Project 2: The Severity Inventory

**Question:** How severe are the firm's typical evidential tests?

**Method:** Take 10 recent analyses (investment theses, research assessments, podcast arguments). For each, ask: "If the conclusion were wrong, would the evidence-gathering method have detected this?" Rate severity on a 0-1 scale. Identify the most common severity failures.

**Output:** A severity scorecard with recommendations for increasing test severity across all faculties.

### Project 3: The Coherence Map

**Question:** Are the firm's aims, methods, and theories coherent in Laudan's sense?

**Method:** Explicitly state: (a) What the firm aims to achieve (its goals across all faculties), (b) What methods it currently uses to achieve those goals, (c) What theoretical commitments underlie those methods. Check for misalignment: are there goals that the current methods can't serve? Are there methods that the current theories don't support?

**Output:** A reticulation diagram showing the relationships between aims, methods, and theories, with misalignments highlighted.

### Project 4: The NFL Domain Map

**Question:** For each of the firm's faculties, which methodologies are reliable and which are unreliable?

**Method:** For each faculty (investing, media, research, publishing), identify the 3-5 most-used reasoning methods (e.g., analogical reasoning, base-rate analysis, first-principles decomposition). For each method-faculty pair, assess: (a) What class of problems does this method solve well? (b) Where does it fail? (c) What problem structures make it unreliable?

**Output:** A matrix mapping methods to faculties to reliability scores, with failure modes annotated.

### Project 5: The Feyerabend Challenge

**Question:** Is Feyerabend right? Is "anything goes" a better description of how the firm actually makes progress than any formal methodology?

**Method:** Take the firm's 5 best insights or decisions over the last year. For each, trace the actual reasoning process. Did it follow any formal methodology? Or did it arise from intuition, serendipity, analogy, or pure creativity? If the best insights didn't follow methodology, what does this imply for the meta-methodological project?

**Output:** An honest assessment of the role of methodology vs. non-methodological insight in the firm's actual intellectual practice.

### Project 6: The Self-Application Test

**Question:** Does the firm's meta-methodology survive self-application?

**Method:** Apply the five criteria (progressivity, severity, coherence, compressibility, domain sensitivity) to the meta-methodology itself. Does the meta-methodology generate novel predictions? Is it severely testable? Is it coherent with its own aims? Is it compressible? Does it acknowledge its own domain limitations?

**Output:** A formal self-evaluation with identified weaknesses and proposed improvements.

### Project 7: The Tacit Knowledge Boundary

**Question:** Where does the firm's knowledge resist formalization?

**Method:** Interview each founder about their decision-making process. Identify cases where the reasoning cannot be articulated in explicit steps. Categorize the tacit knowledge: is it pattern recognition? Aesthetic judgment? Embodied skill? Determine whether this tacit knowledge can be approximated computationally or whether it represents a genuine limit of formalization (per Polanyi).

**Output:** A map of the firm's explicit vs. tacit knowledge, with implications for what can and cannot be built into software.

### Project 8: The Compression Experiment

**Question:** Can the firm's entire intellectual framework be compressed into a small number of principles?

**Method:** Collect all of the firm's stated principles, beliefs, methods, and commitments. Attempt to derive them from the smallest possible set of axioms. How many independent axioms are needed? Is the number shrinking over time (convergence toward deeper principles) or growing (accumulation of ad hoc commitments)?

**Output:** The firm's "axiomatic core" — the smallest set of principles from which everything else follows.

### Journals and Periodicals
- *Philosophy of Science* — The premier journal on scientific methodology
- *Synthese* — Broad epistemology and methodology of science
- *British Journal for the Philosophy of Science* — Rigorous analytical philosophy of science
- *Erkenntnis* — Logic, epistemology, and philosophy of science
- *Studies in History and Philosophy of Science* — Historical case studies in methodological change

### Online Resources
- **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy** (plato.stanford.edu) — Peer-reviewed, authoritative entries on every topic mentioned above. Start with entries on: Scientific Method, Epistemology, Bayesian Epistemology, Belief Revision, Coherentism, Reliabilism, Formal Epistemology.
- **LessWrong** (lesswrong.com) — Community focused on rationality as methodology. Uneven quality but the core sequences are excellent.
- **Metaculus** (metaculus.com) — Prediction platform for calibrating confidence. Practice methodological forecasting.
- **PhilPapers** (philpapers.org) — Comprehensive bibliography of philosophy. Search "methodology" + any topic.

### Podcasts and Lectures
- **Sean Carroll's Mindscape** — Frequently engages with epistemological and methodological questions
- **The Royal Institution lectures** — On scientific method and reasoning
- **MIT OpenCourseWare: Logic and Philosophy of Science courses** — Free, rigorous

- **Center for Philosophy of Science, University of Pittsburgh** — The premier US program
- **Formal Epistemology Workshop** (annual) — Cutting-edge formal work on methodology

## PART V: A NOTE ON HOW TO READ

The point of these readings is not to adopt any single framework. It is to develop what Isaiah Berlin called "the sense of reality" — the ability to navigate between competing frameworks, understand each on its own terms, and judge which applies where.

Each founder should emerge with a DIFFERENT conception of meta-methodology. That disagreement is itself an instance of the meta-method: if the founders all arrive at the same conclusion, they haven't tested the idea severely enough. The firm's strength is not consensus but productive disagreement grounded in shared criteria for what counts as a good argument.

Read adversarially. For every text, ask: "What would this author's strongest critic say?" Then read that critic. Then ask: "What would a critic of the critic say?" This process — which is just the Dialectical Crucible in human form — is itself the meta-method in action.
